Programmed to Kill: The Politics of Serial Murder by David McGowan

2024-02-22
2 minute read
library
nonfiction

Programmed to Kill was an unsettling yet eye-opening experience that forced me to reconsider everything I thought I knew about serial killers and the broader societal forces at play. David McGowan’s core argument—that many infamous serial murders might not be the work of lone, psychopathic individuals but instead tied to larger, organised conspiracies—left me grappling with some profoundly uncomfortable questions.

The Myth of the “Lone Wolf”

One of the most striking revelations from the book is how the archetype of the solitary, deranged killer is rarely questioned. McGowan explores how media and law enforcement reinforce this image, even when evidence suggests something far more complex. For instance, he highlights bizarre inconsistencies in high-profile cases, such as unusual access to victims, suspicious ties to military or intelligence organisations, and unexplained lapses in police investigations.

This challenged me to see serial murder not just as isolated tragedies but as potentially interconnected events influenced by larger, unseen forces. It was startling to realise how easily public perception can be shaped to accept tidy explanations, even when those explanations don’t hold up to scrutiny.

Patterns in the Backgrounds of Killers

Another key takeaway was the chilling pattern McGowan identifies in the backgrounds of many alleged killers. He points out connections to military service, intelligence agencies, or elite institutions that seem too consistent to dismiss as coincidence. This raised questions for me about how such environments might contribute to the making of a killer—or even how such individuals might be exploited for darker purposes.

It made me reflect on how much we don’t know—or aren’t told—about the systems that produce violence in society. Could these patterns indicate grooming, manipulation, or complicity on a larger scale? McGowan doesn’t provide definitive answers, but he certainly raises questions worth asking.

The Role of Institutions

What struck me most was McGowan’s exploration of institutional complicity. He argues that many serial killers may operate with a degree of protection, whether intentional or through negligence. He links some cases to secret societies, government operations, and even covert mind control programmes, drawing parallels between the seemingly unconnected.

While some of these claims felt speculative, they forced me to think critically about the narratives we are presented with. How much of what we know about crime—and who commits it—is shaped by powerful entities with their own agendas? McGowan suggests that the answer may be darker than we’re comfortable admitting.

A New Lens on True Crime

Finally, this book reshaped how I approach true crime stories. Instead of accepting surface-level details or “official” conclusions, I now feel more inclined to question the gaps and contradictions in these accounts. Who benefits from the dominant narrative? What evidence isn’t being discussed?

Closing Thoughts

Programmed to Kill wasn’t so much a set of concrete facts as a new way of seeing. McGowan doesn’t always provide airtight proof, and some of his theories feel like they reach beyond what’s verifiable. But his work taught me to look deeper—to question how stories are told and who gets to tell them.

Whether or not you agree with McGowan’s conclusions, his book is a masterclass in critical thinking and scepticism. It reminded me of the power of asking hard questions, even when the answers might lead to uncomfortable truths.
